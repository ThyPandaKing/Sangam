How this is built

The logic, in one breath

Sangam is the rare side project that touches almost the entire distributed-systems syllabus: connectors (API design, retries, idempotency), Flows (scheduling, event-driven architecture, queues), typed Actions (type systems, validation), multi-tenancy + IAM/RBAC (auth, isolation, data modeling), per-flow logs (observability), and an AI assistant (applied LLM/RAG). So instead of learning theory and building separately, each quarter you learn the concept, then ship the part of Sangam that forces you to use it. That's the fastest path to depth — and it produces a real product you can put users on, which is the founder half of your goal.

One honest constraint: "good depth in all domains" in 2 years isn't real. This plan makes you deep in distributed systems (your edge), functional in applied AI, frontend, and product, and literate in business. Founders win by knowing one thing cold and enough about the rest to make good calls and hire well.

From people who made the jump

Five mentality shifts to run continuously

These aren't quarterly tasks — they're lenses to apply to every piece of work, all eight quarters. Drawn from staff engineers at Pinterest, Nubank, and the Staff Engineer / Staff Engineer's Path literature.

01

Solve problems, not puzzles

The dopamine of cracking a hard bug is not the same as moving a business outcome. Before going deep, ask: whose problem is this, and is it worth solving? Changing the behavior of 1000 engineers beats changing 1000 lines of code — and as a founder, problem-selection is the job.

02

Build for multiplicative impact

The defining staff behavior is work that benefits dozens of others — tooling, standards, removing recurring pain. The trap is staying heads-down on individual output because it feels productive.

03

Train mindset over collecting skills

Depth builds intuition: enough reps that you can predict how a system behaves without a proof-of-concept. You get there by experimenting and collecting fast feedback, not by hoarding tutorials.

04

Simplicity is the harder craft

Anyone can make it complicated. Treat your first solution as a draft, then spend equal time making it simpler — fewer moving parts, fewer files, fewer things to maintain. With a tiny team this is survival, not aesthetics.

05

Be the engineer everyone wants to work with

The durable success metric. It holds whether you're chasing a promotion or recruiting your first cofounder. Reputation compounds; it's the raw material of both.

Always-on

Six habits to install now

Attach each to an existing routine so it survives busy sprints. Motion (setting a goal) is easy; action (a repeatable trigger) is what compounds.

Flag + fix, never just flag

"X is slow" gets ignored. "X is slow, here's the data, here's the fix, I'll help" gets action. Reduce friction enough that someone can act on it today.

drove cross-team impact pre-promotion

Ship in public, weekly

A short write-up, a Sangam dev-log, a demo. Opportunities follow people who share useful things. This is also founder credibility being banked early.

writing finds the holes in your understanding

Define the bar with a sponsor

Ask your manager: "If I were promoted in 1–2 years, what would justify it?" Find a sponsor who advocates, not just a mentor who advises.

calibrate to what's actually rewarded

One design doc a month

Write the doc before the code for anything non-trivial in Sangam or at work. A doc a team could execute without you in the room is the staff artifact.

simplicity + influence in one habit

Talk to a user every 2 weeks

Even one. Build empathy for the person on the other side of the screen — at first your "users" are other engineers; later they pay you.

the most-skipped, highest-leverage habit

Learn by building, not consuming

Every book/course must produce something that forces the concept into your hands within a week. Passive learning rots; built knowledge sticks.
